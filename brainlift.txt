PURPOSE

Figure out where AI actually helps a learner and where it gets in the way, so that the
interactive practice teaches the subject on its own and AI only makes it better. My
working belief: the big wins come from learning science and from feeding the AI the
lesson's real data, not from a bigger model or a chatbot.

In Scope: teaching one subject deeply through hands-on, instantly graded problems;
deciding which AI features earn their place; the learning-science ideas that make
practice stick.

Out of Scope: a general chatbot that answers anything; letting the model decide if an
answer is right; covering many subjects shallowly.


DOK 4: SPIKY POINTS OF VIEW

SPOV 1: A chatbot tutor is the wrong first AI feature.
Clicking the right multiple-choice option feels like progress, but that's recognition,
not learning. What sticks is having to pull an idea out of your own head and getting
corrected when you miss. So the smarter use of AI is a review that builds new problems
out of the ones you already got wrong, not a bot sitting there waiting for you to ask
it something. And those problems have to be actually new. If it's the same question
with the numbers swapped, the student just memorizes the answer.

SPOV 2: A bigger model won't fix a bad tutor. Better context will.
My tutor was useless until I stopped handing it the page text and started handing it
the real problem data and the known answer (flagged as private), plus chapter notes I
had researched. Same model, completely different quality. Most "we need a smarter
model" conversations are really "we never gave the model the right context"
conversations.

SPOV 3: AI can finally give every kid a private tutor, and that should scare textbook publishers.
Bloom showed in 1984 that a student with a one-on-one tutor ends up near the top 2% of
a normal class. We have known the answer for forty years and ignored it, because you
cannot hire a tutor for every child. AI can. It can give each learner a patient tutor
that keeps inventing the exact next problem they need and never runs out. Once that
works, a fixed textbook with the same 30 problems for everyone looks silly. I built a
small version of it in a week by myself, which tells you where this is headed.


EXPERTS

Robert Bjork (UCLA). "Desirable difficulties": spacing, interleaving, and retrieval
feel harder but teach more. Backs the mistake-driven, spaced review.
https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/

Roediger and Karpicke (WashU, Purdue). The testing effect: recalling beats rereading.
Why I favor active recall and resurfacing missed items. Roediger and Karpicke (2006),

John Sweller (UNSW). Cognitive Load Theory and the worked-example effect: novices
learn well from fully worked examples. Why the tutor solves its own examples and the
screens stay uncluttered. Sweller (1988), Cognitive Science.

Benjamin Bloom and Kurt VanLehn. One-to-one mastery tutoring beats group teaching by a
lot (the 2 sigma result), and good software tutors get close. The bar for
personalization. Bloom (1984), Educational Researcher; VanLehn (2011).


DOK 3: INSIGHTS

- Resurfacing wrong answers is the cheapest big win. It is retrieval practice, spacing,
  and error review all at once, with almost no UI.
- Regenerated problems have to be new scenarios, not renumbered copies, or you train
  memorizing an answer instead of the skill.
- "Lesson as data, not HTML" is the keystone. Typed steps are what give instant grading
  and also what give the AI real state to ground on.
- Grade with code, never the model. Deterministic grading plus checking the AI's output
  is what makes it safe to put in front of a learner.


DOK 2: KNOWLEDGE TREE

Retrieval practice
  Roediger and Karpicke (2006), Psychological Science.
  Facts: tested students recalled far more on a one-week-later test than students who
  restudied; the gap grew with time; restudying raised confidence but not retention.
  Summary: testing is a learning event, not just a measurement. Favor recall and reuse
  of missed items.
  https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x

Spacing and interleaving
  Rohrer and Taylor (2007), Instructional Science.
  Facts: students who interleaved problem types scored about 63% vs 20% on a delayed
  test; it felt harder during practice; the gain is learning to pick the right method.
  Summary: mix problem types in review instead of blocking them.
  https://doi.org/10.1007/s11251-007-9015-8

Cognitive load
  Sweller (1988), Cognitive Science.
  Facts: working memory is limited; worked examples often beat unguided problem solving
  for novices; visual clutter competes with learning.
  Summary: give novices a worked example and keep the interface clean.
  https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1207/s15516709cog1202_4

Tutoring and mastery
  Bloom (1984), Educational Researcher.
  Facts: one-to-one mastery tutoring moved the average student to about the 98th
  percentile (two standard deviations), using feedback-and-correct cycles.
  Summary: personalized mastery is the goal; immediate feedback and targeted practice
  are the scalable parts.
  https://doi.org/10.3102/0013189X013006004

Learn-by-doing
  Freeman et al. (2014), PNAS.
  Facts: across 225 studies, active learning raised exam scores about 6% and cut
  failure rates from 34% to 22% versus lecture.
  Summary: doing beats watching. Build the interactive MVP first, add AI later.
  https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1319030111
